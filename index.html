My background and the Origin Story

Hello, My name is Elad, I am 39 years old and live in Kiryat Shmona. I love nature and animals, and for as long as I can remember, I have been specifically interested in dinosaurs. Naturally, I also loved anything related to them, such as the Jurassic park/world movies. This is also why my collection is not limited to any one specific category.
My collection evolved almost on its own; I used to collect dinosaur figures and items from the movies, some of which I still have today. As I grew up, the world changed too,and access to items became much easier. So today, the collection contains items that I never imagined I could obtain as a child.
Just as there are always new discoveries and new fossils, the collection continues to grow and my curiosity deepens. I created the site to show some of the items I've collected over the years, and perhaps to spark interest in others through these preserved items and the stories they tell.

What's in my collection?

There is an unwritten rule in collecting that it is better to focus on one category, but my collection is the exact opposite- more like "eclectic". I collect dinosaur models, trading cards, LEGO sets, booklets, comics, Funko Pops, fossils, and more—simply because I find everything special and valuable. A piece can also hold personal nostalgic value (like items I’ve kept since childhood), and that’s something you can’t define by a category. Of course, one of my goals is to complete full sets (for example, every card box from every movie), but I truly enjoy the collection itself, what it represents, and the combination of all the different items.



About the movies

The Jurassic Park Trilogy (1993–2001)

Focused on the initial experiments and the immediate fallout of bringing dinosaurs back to life.

  • Jurassic Park (1993): Billionaire John Hammond invites a group of experts to a remote island to "endorse" his new theme park. After a saboteur shuts down the power, the dinosaurs escape, and the group must survive a trek across the island.
  • The Lost World: Jurassic Park (1997):Ian Malcolm is sent to "Site B" (Isla Sorna), a second island where the dinosaurs have been living in the wild. He must stop a corporate team from capturing the animals and bringing them to the mainland.
  • Jurassic Park III (2001): Dr. Alan Grant is tricked into visiting Isla Sorna by a couple searching for their missing son. This entry is known for introducing the Spinosaurus, which famously challenges the T-Rex for dominance.

The Jurassic World Trilogy (2015–2022)

Featuring a fully functional, successful theme park that eventually collapses.

  • Jurassic World (2015): The park Hammond dreamed of is finally open and thriving. However, to boost attendance, scientists create a genetically modified hybrid, the Indominus rex, which escapes and causes the park's permanent closure.
  • Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom (2018): Three years after the park's demise, Owen Grady and Claire Dearing return to the island to rescue the dinosaurs from an active volcano. The survivors are brought to the U.S., where they are eventually released into the wild.
  • Jurassic World: Dominion (2022): Set four years after dinosaurs were released into the world. It brings together the original cast (Grant, Sattler, Malcolm) and the new cast (Owen, Claire) to stop a corporate conspiracy involving prehistoric locusts and the ecological impact of dinosaurs living among humans.

The New Era (2025+)

  • Jurassic World Rebirth (2025):It follows a team attempting to secure DNA samples from the world's three largest remaining dinosaurs in an isolated equatorial environment.

The franchise remains a titan of pop culture, famously sparking a massive surge in public interest in paleontology (the "Jurassic Park effect") and setting the gold standard for visual effects in Hollywood.

Movies vs Reality: fun facts

The Jurassic franchises are responsible for shaping how the world views dinosaurs, but Hollywood's version of these creatures often prioritizes "movie monster" aesthetics over paleontological accuracy. Since the first film in 1993, our scientific understanding of dinosaurs has shifted dramatically.

  1. The Presence of Feathers: This is the most significant discrepancy. In the movies, almost all dinosaurs have "lizard-like" scaly skin. In reality, we now know that a vast number of theropods (the two-legged carnivores) were covered in feathers.
    • Velociraptors: Real Velociraptors were roughly the size of a large turkey and were completely covered in bird-like feathers, including "wing" feathers on their arms.
    • The T-Rex: While adult Rexes may have been mostly scaly due to their massive size (to prevent overheating), many of its smaller ancestors and potentially its juveniles likely had patches of "proto-feathers" resembling peach fuzz.

  2. Size Distortions: The movies often "upscale" dinosaurs to make them more intimidating (Velociraptor,Mosasaurs) or "downscale" them (Dilophosaurus) to fit into tight corridors.

  3. Physical Anatomy and Posture: Hollywood often tweaks the "chassis" of these animals to make them look more aggressive.
    • The "Bunny Hands": In the movies, theropods like the T-Rex and Raptor are often shown with their palms facing downward (like they are playing a piano). In reality, dinosaur wrists were not built to rotate that way. Their palms would have faced each other, much like a person clapping or a bird folding its wings.
    • The Dilophosaurus: The movie version is famous for two things: a colorful neck frill and the ability to spit venom, but there is absolutely no fossil evidence that any dinosaur spit venom, and the neck frill was a purely creative choice by the filmmakers to make the creature unique.

  4. Sensory Abilities and Behavior:
    • "Don't Move": In the first film, it’s claimed that the T-Rex’s vision is "based on movement." Paleontology suggests the opposite. The T-Rex had massive optic nerves and forward-facing eyes, giving it excellent binocular vision and depth perception—likely better than a modern hawk.
    • Vocalizations: That iconic T-Rex roar? It was created by mixing sounds from a baby elephant, a tiger, and an alligator. Real dinosaurs likely didn't "roar" in the mammalian sense. They probably made low-frequency booms, hisses, or closed-mouth vocalizations similar to crocodiles or ostriches.

But why the difference?
To be fair to the films, the 1993 original was actually quite scientifically accurate for its time. In the film's direction, they even consulted with paleontologists. Since then, the franchise has used "genetic engineering" as an in-universe explanation for the inaccuracies. As Dr. Henry Wu says in Jurassic World:
"Nothing in Jurassic World is natural, we have always filled gaps in the genome with the DNA of other animals. And if the genetic code was pure, many of them would look quite different. But you didn't ask for reality, you asked for more teeth."

care and preservation

To ensure a collection remains in pristine condition for years to come, I am taking several steps:
The first basic step is to maintain proper humidity and temperature levels in the room (approximately 50% humidity and 24°C). To achieve this, I use an electric dehumidifier, which also includes an integrated electric Air furifier(dust collector).
The second step is to protect the items from UV radiation. To do this, I ensure the items are kept in a place shielded from the sun; some are also kept inside acrylic display boxes or a flexible plastic accessory called a "toploader" (primarily for cards, booklets, and paper products).
The third step is to use special acid-free packaging or sleeves.this is crucial for archival preservation. Acid causes yellowing and the transfer of ink from the item to the wrapper.

QA connection

One of the reasons I chose to study QA is that the field fits my personality, much like that of a collector. The common denominator between them is being meticulous, identifying inaccuracies and potential flaws, striving for a perfect result, and maintaining it.
In collecting,"Mint" grade is the highest level, and it rates an authentic item that meets specific ideal standards (each category has its own) without any defects. The field of QA demands those same high standards from me; only instead of looking for flaws in a trading card, I am looking for them in the code.
